Technical analysis is a method of evaluating securities by analyzing statistics generated by market activity, such as past prices and volume. This guide covers the principles, tools, and strategies of technical analysis.

Introduction to Technical Analysis

Technical analysis is based on the premise that historical price action can help predict future price movements. Unlike fundamental analysis, which examines a company's financial health and business prospects, technical analysis focuses on price patterns, trading signals, and various analytical charting tools.

Core Principles of Technical Analysis
  • Price Discounts Everything: Technical analysts believe that all known information is reflected in the price
  • Prices Move in Trends: Prices are more likely to continue a past trend than move randomly
  • History Tends to Repeat: Market patterns and behaviors tend to recur over time

Chart Types

Charts are the primary tools of technical analysis. Different chart types provide various perspectives on price movements.

Line Charts

The simplest form of chart that connects closing prices over time. Useful for identifying overall trends but provides limited information.

Bar Charts

Displays high, low, open, and closing prices for each time period. Offers more information than a line chart while remaining relatively simple.

Candlestick Charts

Japanese charting method that shows the same price information as bar charts, but with a more visual representation that highlights the relationship between opening and closing prices.

Point and Figure Charts

Focuses exclusively on price movements, filtering out time and minor price changes. Useful for identifying support and resistance levels.

Trend Analysis

Identifying and following trends is a fundamental aspect of technical analysis.

Trend Type Description Technical Identification
Uptrend Series of higher highs and higher lows Price above moving averages; rising trendlines
Downtrend Series of lower highs and lower lows Price below moving averages; falling trendlines
Sideways/Range-bound Price moves horizontally between support and resistance Price oscillating around flat moving averages

Trendlines

Trendlines are straight lines drawn on a chart connecting price points to visualize the direction of price movement. They serve as dynamic support and resistance levels.

  • Upward Trendline: Connects consecutive higher lows in an uptrend
  • Downward Trendline: Connects consecutive lower highs in a downtrend

Support and Resistance

Support and resistance are price levels where a security has historically had difficulty moving below (support) or above (resistance).

Support Levels

Price levels where buying interest is strong enough to overcome selling pressure, preventing further price decline. When broken, support often becomes resistance.

Resistance Levels

Price levels where selling pressure overcomes buying interest, preventing further price increases. When broken, resistance often becomes support.

Psychological Levels

Round numbers (e.g., $50, $100, $1000) often act as psychological support and resistance levels due to their prominence in traders' minds.

Chart Patterns

Chart patterns are distinctive formations on price charts that can signal trend continuations or reversals.

  • Head and Shoulders: Indicates a potential trend reversal from bullish to bearish
  • Inverse Head and Shoulders: Indicates a potential trend reversal from bearish to bullish
  • Double Top: Looks like the letter "M" and signals a potential downward trend
  • Double Bottom: Looks like the letter "W" and signals a potential upward trend
  • Triple Top/Bottom: Similar to double patterns but with three peaks or troughs
  • Rounding Bottom (Saucer): A long-term reversal pattern indicating a gradual shift from bearish to bullish

  • Flags and Pennants: Short-term consolidation patterns that usually continue in the direction of the previous trend
  • Triangles (Symmetrical, Ascending, Descending): Show a convergence of support and resistance, signaling potential breakouts
  • Rectangles: Occur when prices bounce between parallel support and resistance lines
  • Cup and Handle: A bullish continuation pattern resembling a teacup with a handle on the right side

Bullish Patterns
  • Hammer: Small body with a long lower shadow, signals potential reversal in a downtrend
  • Bullish Engulfing: A large bullish candle that completely engulfs the previous bearish candle
  • Morning Star: Three-candle pattern indicating a potential bottom
Bearish Patterns
  • Hanging Man: Resembles a hammer but appears in an uptrend, signaling potential reversal
  • Bearish Engulfing: A large bearish candle that completely engulfs the previous bullish candle
  • Evening Star: Three-candle pattern indicating a potential top

Technical Indicators

Technical indicators are mathematical calculations based on price, volume, or open interest that can help identify market conditions and potential entry/exit points.

  • Moving Averages: Average price over a specific time period, smooths out price data
  • Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD): Relationship between two moving averages of a security's price
  • Parabolic SAR: Designed to identify potential reversal points in the price movement
  • Average Directional Index (ADX): Measures the strength of a trend, regardless of its direction

  • Relative Strength Index (RSI): Measures the speed and change of price movements, indicating overbought or oversold conditions
  • Stochastic Oscillator: Compares a security's closing price to its price range over a specific period
  • Williams %R: Similar to the Stochastic Oscillator but measures overbought/oversold levels
  • Rate of Change (ROC): Measures the percentage change in price over a specific time period

  • On-Balance Volume (OBV): Relates volume to price change, showing cumulative buying or selling pressure
  • Volume Profile: Shows trading activity at specific price levels
  • Chaikin Money Flow: Measures buying and selling pressure over a set period
  • Accumulation/Distribution Line: Uses price and volume to determine if investors are accumulating (buying) or distributing (selling)

  • Bollinger Bands: Consists of a middle band (usually a 20-period moving average) with upper and lower bands typically set at 2 standard deviations
  • Average True Range (ATR): Measures market volatility by decomposing the entire range of a security's price for that period
  • Keltner Channels: Similar to Bollinger Bands but uses Average True Range (ATR) for band calculations
  • Standard Deviation: Measures the dispersion of a dataset relative to its mean

Trading Strategies Using Technical Analysis

Trend Following

Trading in the direction of the established trend. Typically uses moving averages, trendlines, and other trend indicators to identify entry and exit points.

Range Trading

Trading between support and resistance levels in sideways markets. Often uses oscillators to identify overbought and oversold conditions.

Breakout Trading

Entering trades when price breaks through established support or resistance levels, indicating potential for continued movement in the breakout direction.

Reversal Trading

Looking for signs that a trend is exhausting and about to reverse. Uses chart patterns and candlestick formations to identify potential reversal points.

Technical Analysis Time Frames

Time Frame Chart Interval Typical Trading Style Considerations
Short-term 1-minute to 1-hour charts Day trading, scalping Higher transaction costs, requires constant monitoring, more prone to noise
Medium-term 4-hour to daily charts Swing trading Balance between noise and trend identification, less time-intensive
Long-term Weekly to monthly charts Position trading Focuses on major trends, less affected by market noise, requires patience

Limitations of Technical Analysis

  • Self-fulfilling prophecy: Popular patterns and indicators may work partially because many traders are watching and acting on them
  • Subjective interpretation: Different analysts may interpret the same chart differently
  • Lagging indicators: Many technical indicators are based on past price data and may lag current market conditions
  • Market efficiency challenges: If markets are efficient, past price information should not predict future movements
  • External factors: Unexpected news events or economic data can render technical setups invalid

Best Practices for Technical Analysis

Use Multiple Time Frames

Analyze longer time frames first to identify the major trend, then use shorter time frames to fine-tune entry and exit points.

Combine Multiple Indicators

Use different types of indicators together (e.g., trend, momentum, and volume) to confirm signals and reduce false positives.

Consider Fundamental Factors

Combine technical analysis with fundamental analysis for a more comprehensive view of potential price movements.

Practice Risk Management

Always use stop-loss orders and position sizing to manage risk, regardless of how strong a technical signal appears.
